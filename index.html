Hi, and welcome to my Grand Challenges Scholars Program portfolio! My name is Daniel Personius and I am a computer science undergraduate at the Colorado School of Mines in Golden, Colorado. I will be graduating this fall before moving to San Diego be a data scientist for the communications company Viasat. As my final semester comes to a close, I would like to reflect on my time at Mines and how this program has shaped it.

For readers who may not know, The GCSP is an outcomes-based at eighty-one schools across many countries. It aims to enhance engineering students’ social, political, economic, and social awareness and skills, preparing them to take on the National Academy of Engineering’s Grand Challenges of Engineering. These are fourteen large-scale issues facing the 21st century, spanning borders, professions, and cultures. Problems like preventing nuclear terror, ensuring access to clean water, reverse engineering the brain, and engineering the tools for scientific discovery. Problems that we as a global society need to address in order to maintain our health, joy, and sustainability as a species. At Mines, the scholars program has students complete five pillars to educate them in more than just math and science: Interdisciplinary Curriculum, a Global Dimension, Entrepreneurship, Service Learning, and Research or Project Experience.

Throughout the program, I have had many amazing opportunities to gain knowledge and experience in these areas of expertise. Next is a brief summary of each activity, followed by reflections, a synthesis piece, and my feedback on the program as a whole.

For my interdisciplinary experience, I completed two activities. My freshman year, I worked with a team of chemical and mechanical engineers on a term project for a Nature and Human Values course. This course combined design and philosophy to introduce students to the core ideas of human-centered engineering. Over the course of a semester, we studied the living situations of the homeless population in Denver, Colorado, then proposed a product to improve those conditions. The problem we saw as most pressing at the time was homeless individuals’ access to nutritional food, mostly due to the high prices in the city and overcrowded and underfunded shelters. To mitigate this, we designed a donation system for restaurants and other places of business to transfer uneaten or extra food into distributed vending machines to be bought by homeless and low-income individuals.

Later on during my senior year, I entered a design competition with a team of fellow Scholars focused on the Grand Challenges. We had three months to design a product from scratch, create a company around it, and compete against teams from across the United States in Irvine, California. This winners of this stage moved on to pitch at the global student collaboration lab preceding the 2019 EGC Global Summit in London. We did not move onto this final round, but our company Canary has lived on. Canary a patent-pending design for integrating personal air pollution masks into everyday clothing. Traditional masks are often avoided due to inconvenience and negative stigma in many countries, so we made a way for the important part of the mask, the air filter, to fit into more comfortable and appealing clothing, like Buffs, scarves, and jackets. This project was a wonderful experience for me — I had the chance to work on a product not built with code, learn from my peers of metallurgical, mechanical, chemical, and electrical engineers, and apply knowledge and skills learned from my Entrepreneurship pillar.

My Entrepreneurship competency was filled by co-founding a company my freshman year. Through a friend, I met a local business-owner who had an idea for a professional services startup and was in need of both a development team and people to run the business and refine his idea. For two and a half years, I worked to develop the technology for the company, as well as hire other developers, speak with legal consultants and potential investors, run beta tests with potential customers, write business plans and overview documents, as well as give market reports. I left the company while it was still in development due to school and work constraints, but in my time there, I gained a more clear understanding of how companies start and become successful, but more importantly, how and why they can fail.

The Service Learning Pillar is an opportunity to address problems that impact people and their communities, preparing students to better handle the social dimensions inherent to Grand Challenges.

As a sophomore, I volunteered at the Excel Academy in Arvada, Colorado teaching an after-school robotics and programming class to 7th and 8th graders. We met once a week for a semester, each time with a lesson on fundamental programming concepts and an open-ended circuit build.

I interned this summer as a software engineer for Viasat, a broadband communications company based in Carlsbad, California. At an internal hackathon, our team of software engineering, data analytics, marketing, and business interns developed a machine learning model to better predict customer churn and turnover, providing possible discounts and plan changes for customer care agents to suggest to dissatisfied customers.

The Global pillar aims at bolstering the understanding of different cultures to ensure cultural acceptance of proposed engineering solutions. At Mines, I achieved this through both in-class and hands-on experiences.

My junior year, I enrolled in Asia: Politics and Society, a course at Mines taught by Professor James Jesudason. It highlighted the social, political, economic, and cultural environments of Eastern and Southeastern asian countries. As a final project, I wrote a research paper exploring how Myanmar’s Rohingya refugee crisis is influenced and fueled in part by social media platforms like Facebook and Twitter. Myanmar has been plagued for decades with violent conflict between their military and various ethnic and religious groups. Social media in no way induced the struggle, but in recent years, has been a powerful tool for the military to spread misinformation, cultivating a collective hatred for these minorities.

In the fall of 2019, my senior year, I attended the Global Grand Challenges Summit Student Collaboration Lab in London. There I competed in a one day design competition on a team of students from China, the U.K., and the U.S. We designed a data management system for the healthcare industry based on Blockchain technology and the Ethereum network.

The project-based pillar is a chance for students to unify and apply the skills they have learned from the Scholar Program to a Grand Challenges-related problem.

During the fall of 2019, I have worked with a group of students from Mines to build a virtual reality application for Lockheed Martin. This application is for visualizing data from space object catalogs and alerting users to potential space events, like pending collisions. It consists of a global view, showing space objects and observation sites from an in-orbit perspective, a ground view that has the user look up from those observation sites, and various data-driven views for aggregating information regarding those objects, as well as monitoring events. In the future, the program will be expanded to allow the user to directly adjust orbits and send instructions to satellites.

Space object visualization and manipulation are related to the Grand Challenges in that applications such as our own contribute to the Challenge of Engineering the Tools for Scientific Discovery. Lockheed Martin was previously was using Tableau for visualization, a powerful visualization tool, but not well suited for satellite data. By giving the company and its clients more engaging and intuitive insight into their data, they can better understand and act on it, thus more efficiently and effectively utilizing their satellites for gathering data about and connecting the world.

Going to school for engineering, especially at an all-engineering university, can make it hard to feel connected to the rest of the world. Course content is often divorced from application and lacking a focus on people. I’ve gained valuable skills from school, but have felt they have been put to good use only when applied socially, rather than technically. Teaching as a volunteer at Excel was the first chance I got to do this. University serves to prepare you for a job, but with these kids, I finally wasn't just focusing on how I could make money or grow my own skills. And this reminded me of what the goal of engineering is in the first place — not to exist for its own sake or for the sake of its followers, but to improve people’s lives. Granted, I was teaching the students computer science as a potential career path in the same way Mines taught it to me, but promoting a career never felt like the goal. Instead, I just wanted the kids to better understand the technology-steeped world around them and have fun doing it. Engineerings usually improve quality of life by focusing on the health and convenience of consumers, but less often joy. This class was a refreshing chance to shift that for myself.

Teaching these kids didn’t change my life. It wasn’t the first or greatest experience I’ve have volunteering or giving back to the community. But it was one of the first things I did at Mines that felt connected to the real world. For example, we designed products and programs for the homeless in Nature and Human Values freshman year, but those projects never felt very impactful. They felt like we were looking through glass, deciding for ourselves what we saw and what needed to be done. Teaching at Excel was different. I don’t think I changed any lives, but the students had never had access to programming or robotics classes before, and they seemed to genuinely enjoy the opportunity.



   

Viasat


I have an overall positive experience/impression of social media. It improves my life in small, simple ways, and the few downsides are just first-world problems. But since its inception, social media has directly and indirectly contributed to increased bullying and suicide rates, large-scale misinformation, and in many places like Myanmar, hate crimes and genocide. Before my research for my paper, I was largely unaware of the extent of the damages digital connection can cause. My research also revealed that many of the problems social media can bring emerged long before it was widely used in Myanmar, showing that they are not unique to it. This further suggests a lack of foresight from Western companies and an inability to learn from experience in countries with well-established digital presence. I found this observation particularly applicable to myself as an engineer — it’s easy for me to assume technological innovations will “work” everywhere. It’s easy to view technology as a universal equalizer and as a constant force for good. It’s tempting to automatically throw tech at a problem without considering the possibility that it might actually work against your goals. But, one day in class, Professor Jesudason ended his lecture with a line that has stayed with me: “Equality in opportunity is not equality in outcome.” In this context, I understand this as saying that social, political, and cultural awareness cannot be an afterthought when solving Grand Challenges. Moreover, the successes and failures of previous and current implementations cannot be forgotten when expanding into new markets and countries.



   

Researching and writing the paper on Facebook in Myanmar taught me how tech and innovations can have unintended, unforeseen consequences when local/foreign cultures and sociopolitical climates are not well understood. I, along with my teammates, applied this understanding to Medblock — as we developed our solution, we acknowledged that the health care systems of the world vary wildly, but not always how we expect them to. China and the United States, for instance, actually have very similar methods for the electronic storage, access, and analysis of patient data, which our product could improve. But the distinct social and political forces driving those care systems mean that attempt to uniformly implement our product, not only would our proposal be more often rejected, it could end up failing the patients and providers we set out to help.

It’s obvious to say that things don’t work the same way in other parts of the world as they do where I'm from. I hear it so often that it has almost become white noise. Paradoxically, I have noticed that this fact makes me consider its implications less. MedBlock reminded me that those differences should not just be taken as an implicit assumption at the start of projects, or something you know but never really think about, but as central of a focus as the goal and implementation of the product itself.

At the trade show following our presentation, my team and I talked to a mentor who pointed out our product's potential for helping immigrants, a demographic we hadn't specifically considered. Seeing as many of them are displaced, they don't have the same medical resources, situations, or concerns as do the majority of medical patients in the US. Prior to speaking with him, we had envisioned MedBlock as just working behind the scenes in hospitals to allow them to better manage and analyze patient information, but then realized that it can be used on a much more personal basis — directly by patients. Many people, not just immigrants, don't have all their medical information readily available. Many more don’t have insurance keeping their records or a standard personal identification method, but Blockchain technology could give them a way to carry this information and patient identification with them at all times. This conversation goes to show that not only do differences in cultures and demographics mean your product may slightly change region to region, they may reveal entirely new use cases.


   

Before I had any real experience with engineering and technology on a global scale, I remember thinking that they and the atmosphere surrounding them are a universal language where culture and politics had less influence. This lead me to believe that solutions that work in one country will, with few exceptions, work elsewhere. Politics needs engineering to shape public policy and help lawmakers make effective and informed decisions, but engineering needs an awareness and understanding of politics also, since solutions don't necessarily work across borders.
